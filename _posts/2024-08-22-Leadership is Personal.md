# Leadership is Personal


Two of my juniors have sent me messages, letting me know that they will be leaving my former company. I have been resigned for a while now because we have migrated to another country. I didn't feel like they needed my permission. But I appreciate the gesture. It kinda feels like they look up to me as a leader.

Contrary to probably how they feel about our section head. Which is something that I had to ponder through in order for me to fully grasp the reason why. Was it because of the leadership style? Most of our juniors were very young. And R was very strict when it comes to compliance and delivery. Maybe too strict for the young 'uns who were fresh off college. They might be looking for something more gentler and nurturing. He's not all that.

I, on the other hand, appreciate his leadership style. He is strict enough to keep things in line. And what I really like the most is he acts how I want a leader should be: someone who takes accountability and responsibility for their people. He will never throw you under the bus even though you've been acting like a moron -- like my former teammate. He will reprimand you within the team, but outside of it he will defend you. He is also approachable. Yes. Surprisingly. Maybe it's something the juniors will find hard to believe because they felt intimidated.

I have experienced different type of leadership over the course of my career. The first boss I had was my worst -- which I believe honed my ability to deal with majority of bosses. And I have been lucky to also experience a couple of great bosses these past several years.

When I was starting out, I met JKT. A horrible boss. An even more awful person. He exploits interns by making them work like slaves and not paying them wages. The same goes with his regular employees. He also won't provide you pay stubs (or slips as we call them in the Philippines). He don't provide mandatory benefits. He doesn't pay tax. He verbally abuses all his employees. He'd tell you that a mouse is better than you. If you need a raise you'd have to beg. He micromanages while knowing no shit about what's happening. And more. In retrospect, I probably only stayed because I was a college dropout with nowhere to go to. 

Another notable boss I had was ARA. Other employees remarked that I've already been staying long despite me only being with the team for several months. They believe ARA is difficult to work with, which is shown by the high attrition rate of our developers. But I like how he pushes me beyond what I think my limit is.

When I transferred to BGC, I met Jim. An asshole. He and his senior developer get their high from power tripping. He once got mad at me and a teammate for eating while facing the window during lunch break. Asshole. Majority of my teammates tolerate him. I don't. I just kept my head low while I was working with them. But I have been very vocal about my dislike ever since I left the company.

And then there's CDC. She has been such a great boss. She has helped me get better at management. She also helped me out financially by lobbying for my raise when I was in a pinch because the Taal Volcano erupted. And it was, I think, the first time I felt my boss got my back. I love her. But again, not every one is fond of CDC. She likes and demands quality delivery. She also likes timely delivery. 

So, what am I trying to say? What I'm really getting into is one size does not fit all. One leadership style might be a good fit for someone but at the same time it can also not be beneficial to someone else. 

But here's my take on leadership: I think, at the very least, leaders should be accountable to their people. They also need to be accountable for their people.
